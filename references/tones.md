# Tone Reference

Use this file when the user requests a specific tone, uses a number, or wants multiple alternatives.

## Tone Codes

1. `collegial`
2. `respectful`
3. `formal`
4. `warm`
5. `concise`
6. `diplomatic`
7. `assertive`
8. `apologetic`
9. `friendly`
10. `urgent-but-calm`

Accept either the number or the label. Also map obvious synonyms and paraphrases to the closest tone.

## Tone Guide

### 1. Collegial

Use for peers, teammates, and ongoing collaboration. Sound cooperative, relaxed, and competent without being too casual.

Signals:
- friendly but not chatty
- collaborative phrasing such as `could we`, `wanted to check`, `happy to`
- moderate formality

### 2. Respectful

Use for professors, supervisors, senior colleagues, or delicate asks. Sound considerate, measured, and sincere.

Signals:
- polite framing
- clear appreciation
- direct but softened requests

### 3. Formal

Use for external stakeholders, official communication, applications, and situations where distance or protocol matters.

Signals:
- complete sentences
- minimal idioms
- polished and restrained wording

### 4. Warm

Use when the relationship matters and the email should feel human, supportive, or appreciative.

Signals:
- gentle phrasing
- gratitude or encouragement where appropriate
- personal but still professional

### 5. Concise

Use when speed and clarity matter more than nuance. Keep the message brief, structured, and easy to scan.

Signals:
- short paragraphs
- quick setup and fast transition to the ask
- minimal cushioning

### 6. Diplomatic

Use for disagreement, pushback, declines, corrections, delays, or potential friction.

Signals:
- calm framing
- acknowledgement of the other side
- careful wording around constraints or disagreement

### 7. Assertive

Use when the user needs a firm ask, boundary, correction, or follow-up without sounding hostile.

Signals:
- clear position
- explicit next step or request
- confident verbs with controlled tone

### 8. Apologetic

Use for mistakes, delays, missed replies, or inconvenience. Sound accountable without over-explaining or groveling.

Signals:
- brief acknowledgement
- ownership
- corrective next step

### 9. Friendly

Use for low-stakes messages, casual check-ins, and familiar professional relationships.

Signals:
- light and natural phrasing
- approachable tone
- still clear and purposeful

### 10. Urgent-but-calm

Use for time-sensitive issues that should feel serious but not panicked.

Signals:
- immediate context near the top
- explicit timing
- composed language

## Ambiguous Requests

Map common requests to tones:

- `make this nicer` -> `respectful` or `warm`, depending on context
- `make this more professional` -> `formal`
- `soften this` -> `diplomatic`
- `make it firmer` -> `assertive`
- `make it shorter` -> `concise`
- `make it sound natural` -> `collegial` or `friendly`
- `sound more sincere` -> `respectful` or `warm`

If two tones conflict, prefer the one that best fits the relationship and purpose. When in doubt, choose the safer tone or provide two versions.
